About TicketStub

Every memory deserves
a ticket stub.

TicketStub is an Android app that turns an ordinary photo into a small, collectible keepsake — the kind of thing you'd tuck into a drawer and rediscover years later. It's built for the moments that don't fit neatly into a camera roll: a concert, a first meal at a new restaurant, a train journey, a rainy afternoon worth remembering.

Why a "ticket stub"?

Physical ticket stubs used to do something photos alone don't: they carried context. A date, a venue, a price, a seat number — small printed details that, years later, told you exactly where you were and what it meant. Camera rolls have gotten enormous, but they've lost that context. Everything looks the same: a flat grid of thumbnails with no story attached.

TicketStub brings that context back. Pick a photo, and the app turns it into a stub-style card — with a title, a date, a location, and a short note — styled like something you'd find in an old shoebox of memories. Over time, your stubs build into a personal archive that's actually pleasant to revisit, not just scroll past.

What TicketStub does

At its core, TicketStub is simple: you take or choose a photo, and the app helps you turn it into a memory card with the details that matter — when it happened, where, and why it was worth keeping. Your stubs are organized into a personal collection you can browse, search, and revisit.

  • Photo → Stub: Convert any photo into a ticket-style memory card in a few taps.
  • Context that sticks: Attach a date, location, and personal note to every stub.
  • A real collection: Browse your stubs the way you'd flip through a box of old tickets, not a flat photo grid.
  • Private by default: Your account and stubs are yours — see our Privacy Policy for the details.

Who it's for

TicketStub is for anyone who takes a photo and thinks "I want to remember this properly" — not just have it disappear into a camera roll of four thousand other images. People use it for gig and concert memories, travel days, restaurant visits, small personal milestones, and the kind of ordinary afternoons that turn out to matter more in hindsight than they seemed to at the time.

It's intentionally not a social app. There's no feed, no followers, no public sharing pressure. It's a private, personal archive — built for you to look back on, on your own terms.

Who built it

TicketStub is built and maintained by Pranav Kadam, an independent Android developer based in Pune, India, working under the studio name Daily Tools HQ. It's a solo-built app — designed, developed, and supported by one person, which is why the focus stays narrow: a small set of things done properly, rather than a long feature list done half-heartedly.
